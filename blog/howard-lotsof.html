Howard Lotsof's Gift: The Discovery That Changed Everything

In 1962, a 19-year-old heroin addict named Howard Lotsof took ibogaine — and woke up, 36 hours later, free. His life's work created the global ibogaine treatment movement.

A Young Man and a Chance Discovery

New York City, 1962. Howard Lotsof is 19 years old, a film student, and a heroin addict. Like many young people of his era experimenting with the countercultural underground, he has tried a variety of psychedelic compounds. When a friend offers him a substance called ibogaine — synthesized from the African iboga plant — Lotsof agrees to try it.

What follows is one of the most remarkable stories in the history of medicine. After a 36-hour experience of intense visual imagery, emotional processing, and something he would later describe as a profound encounter with his own life history, Lotsof emerges — and discovers that he has no desire for heroin. No cravings. No withdrawal. The physical dependence that had gripped him is simply gone.

"I woke up and realized I wasn't thinking about heroin. I couldn't remember the last time that had happened." — Howard Lotsof

Expanding the Discovery

Lotsof, a naturally curious and scientifically minded young man, did not keep his discovery to himself. Over the following weeks, he shared ibogaine with several other heroin-dependent friends. Of five additional people he treated, several reported the same dramatic reduction or cessation of withdrawal symptoms and cravings.

This was not placebo. Something pharmacological was occurring — something that no other substance, conventional or otherwise, had ever achieved: the interruption of opioid addiction with a single dose.

A Life Devoted to Ibogaine

Howard Lotsof graduated from college, married, and built a career — but ibogaine never left him. In the 1980s, as the opioid crisis began to emerge in full force, he returned to ibogaine with renewed urgency. Unable to conduct research in the United States — where ibogaine had been classified as Schedule I in 1970 — he moved his work to the Netherlands.

There, Lotsof established the first ibogaine treatment protocols, documenting outcomes systematically and publishing research that introduced ibogaine to the scientific mainstream. He patented methods of ibogaine administration, not to profit from them, but — as he explained — to maintain quality control and prevent dangerous amateur use. His royalties were minimal; his commitment was total.

In 1993, after years of Lotsof's advocacy, the FDA finally approved clinical trials for ibogaine in the United States — the first step toward formal recognition. The trials advanced slowly, hampered by funding shortages and regulatory caution, but the door had been opened.

The Meeting That Shaped History: Lotsof and Wachtel

In the late 1980s, during his years in the United States, Israeli researcher and activist Boaz Wachtel encountered Howard Lotsof and immediately recognized ibogaine's extraordinary potential. The two men formed a powerful partnership. Wachtel began providing ibogaine therapy, and together they produced one of the most important documents in the field: the Manual for Ibogaine Therapy (2003), which codified safe, evidence-based protocols for ibogaine administration and became the standard reference for treatment centers worldwide.

Their collaboration exemplified what ibogaine research has always required: visionary individuals willing to work across disciplines, cultures, and institutional barriers to advance a treatment that the mainstream medical establishment was too cautious — or too invested in existing paradigms — to embrace.

Lotsof's Legacy

Howard Lotsof died in 2010 at the age of 66, having dedicated four decades of his life to ibogaine. He did not live to see the Stanford study, or Texas's $50 million investment, or the growing movement of veterans and families advocating for ibogaine access in the United States, Canada, and Israel. But everything that is happening now traces a direct line back to a young man in New York who woke up one morning, unexpectedly free, and decided to tell the world.

At the Ibogaine Foundation of Israel, we honor Howard Lotsof's legacy every day. The healing we advocate for — of soldiers, survivors, and all those living with the weight of trauma — begins with his gift.
